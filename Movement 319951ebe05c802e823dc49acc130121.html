Movement

Introduction

Movement defines how players navigate the arenas of this high-fantasy, 3rd-person combat experience, blending fluid traversal with tactical depth to enhance both duels and broader engagements. Viewed through an over-the-shoulder perspective, the system ensures every step, dash, or jump feels responsive and purposeful, supporting strategic positioning while tying into combat and character customization. It balances agility with deliberation, offering a range of options from quiet walks to swift sprints, all shaped by a player’s build and resource management.

Goals

The movement system is crafted to:

  • Feel Fluid: Seamlessly carry momentum into attacks, allowing a dodge to flow into a strike or a sprint to transition into a powerful blow.
  • Stay Responsive: Deliver quick, intuitive control for reacting to threats and repositioning, optimized for gamepad accessibility.
  • Enable Positioning: Provide tactical choices—evading into cover, leaping for height, or crouching for stealth—to shape combat outcomes.
  • Support Combat Balance: Maintain a deliberate pace that complements the game’s fighting mechanics, preventing excessive speed from outpacing reactions.
  • Reflect Customization: Adjust movement through character stats, gear weight, and class skills, ensuring diverse playstyles with clear trade-offs.

Overview

At its heart, movement is an 8-way directional system rooted in a deliberate walk—faster than a stroll, slower than a jog—prioritizing precision over haste. This walk speed varies with a character’s build: nimble fighters move quicker, while those in heavy armor tread slower, reflecting their resilience. Two core combat states define the system:

  • Directional State: A wider camera view offers broad awareness, with strafing and backpedaling for adaptable, omnidirectional movement.
  • Aimed State: A tighter, close-up view slows mobility for precision, focusing on aimed attacks and skillshots.

These states anchor a suite of movement options—walking, sprinting, dashing, jumping, crouching, proning, and traversal—each tied to Energy costs that scale with gear and stats. Beyond combat, movement extends to environmental interactions like climbing or vaulting, turning arenas into dynamic spaces for tactical advantage. This creates a system where every action, from a quiet step to a charged leap, enhances both strategy and immersion.

Core Movement States

Directional State

The camera pulls back slightly, offset to the right, providing a wide view to assess threats and surroundings—perfect for spotting an ambush or navigating hazards. Players strafe and backpedal instead of turning to face their movement direction, with only minor slowdowns to keep responsiveness intact. This deliberate pace lets players sidestep melee strikes or circle foes, maintaining awareness in chaotic duels without constant camera adjustments. Attacks in this state are either forward-facing or applied in an omnidirectional way, guided by movement inputs, making it ideal for close-quarters adaptability.

Aimed State

The camera closes in over the shoulder, emphasizing precision for targeting opponents or environmental points. Movement slows to deliberate, even steps with a brief acceleration from neutral, trading speed for accuracy. Special attacks here use motion inputs—such as back-then-forward or quarter-circle movements—triggered by holding and releasing an attack button within a brief window, adding depth to aimed strikes or retreats. This state suits players landing a pinpoint projectile or setting up a charged blow, balancing risk with reward through focused control.

Movement Types and Integration

The system encompasses a variety of movement options, each enhancing combat and traversal:

  • Walking: The default 8-way stance offers precise, Energy-neutral control, quiet enough to avoid detection. Speed adjusts with agility and gear weight—light builds glide faster, heavy ones step with purpose.
  • Sprinting: A faster gait drains Energy continuously, ideal for closing gaps or fleeing, but slows under attack. It shifts to a state with momentum-driven attacks, like a charging strike, balancing speed with vulnerability.
  • Dashing/Dodging: Quick bursts cost Energy, scaled by build—agile characters cover more ground, heavy ones less. These evade strikes or adjust aim, offering reactive flexibility in both states.
  • Jumping: Leaps scale with hold duration, entering a charged state for aerial knockup attacks. Energy costs reflect height and weight, adding verticality to tactics.
  • Crouching: Slows movement, shrinks the hitbox, and enables low attacks or stealth, costing no Energy—perfect for dodging high blows or sneaking into position.
  • Proning: Drops to the ground, limiting mobility but altering vulnerability for defense or low strikes—useful after a knockdown or as a tactical choice.
  • Traversal: Climbing and vaulting scale walls or leap obstacles, costing Energy based on agility and weight. These turn arenas into multi-level battlegrounds for ambushes or escapes.
  • Stealth: A utility option from crouching or proning reduces detection—quiet steps or class skills like invisibility—balanced by Energy and duration limits.

Combat and Build Integration

Movement weaves into combat seamlessly:

  • Energy Costs: Sprinting, dashing, jumping, and traversal drain Energy, scaled by build—light characters pay less, heavy ones more—ensuring strategic choices over reckless evasion.
  • State Synergy: The Directional State’s strafing flows into dashes or dogdes for reactive play, while the Aimed State’s slow steps pair with precise specials—e.g., a backpedal sets up a stalwart retreat attack.
  • Build Impact: Some stats can boost dash distance and jump height, while others might enhance knockback resistance. Gear weight slows heavy builds but bolsters resilience, tailoring movement to each fighter.

Challenges and Solutions

  • Evasion Overload: Fluid movement might favor dodging over fighting, especially for agile builds.
    • Solution: Scale Energy costs by weight and stats—e.g., heavy builds pay more for dashes, light builds drain faster under attack—balancing mobility with combat focus.
  • Precision Lag: The Aimed State’s slower pace could hinder gamepad responsiveness.
    • Solution: Add a soft-lock reticle and buffer motion inputs (5-10 frames), ensuring aimed actions feel fluid—e.g., a quarter-circle special triggers reliably.
  • Build Disparity: Heavy builds might feel sluggish compared to light ones.
    • Solution: Offset with strength benefits—e.g., heavy builds resist knockback and juggle less, light builds evade better but take more damage—keeping all styles viable.

Conclusion

Movement is a dynamic, build-driven system that turns arenas into tactical playgrounds. With Directional and Aimed States anchoring a suite of options—walking, sprinting, dashing, jumping, crouching, proning, and traversal—it empowers players to outmaneuver foes with skill and strategy. Energy costs and customization ensure every action feels deliberate and impactful, enhancing the game’s blend of fighting precision and RPG depth for thrilling, immersive duels.
