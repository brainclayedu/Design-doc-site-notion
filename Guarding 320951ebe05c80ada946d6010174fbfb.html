Guarding

Absolute Guard

A game mechanic that forces the player's character to block all incoming attacks while the player is trapped in block stun, even if the player lets go of the block button. As long as the player's opponent attacks the player with a true block string, the player is just stuck. The player can buffer an attack while in block stun, which will come out immediately after block stun. Absolute guard will continue to block high, low or mid once engaged.

Faultless Guard

While guarding, hold the grab button

Powerful defensive option, but drains Poise as you use it!

Faultless Guard is an interesting and highly useful technique. It's often used to negate chip damage, but it has other important uses.

Faultless Guard is often used to end an attacker's blockstring sooner by pushing them out of attack range. An advanced strategy is to make the attack miss by pushing them away with FD, then punish while the attacker is still recovering from the whiffed attack!

Notable aspects of Faultless Guard

  • Consumes Poise while held (first an initial small chunk then a steady drain while held)
    • Can not Faultless Guard when Poise Gauge is empty
  • Prevents chip damage
  • Does not cost energy
  • Increased pushback for the defender compared to normal blocking
    • Extra pushback increases with Attack Level
    • Projectile pushback is unaffected
  • Air FD increases gravity compared to normal blocking, letting the defender land sooner

Just like with Instant Blocking, you still must guard high/low attacks correctly when using Faultless Guard. Furthermore, you cannot jump or tech throws while using Faultless Guard!

Keep in mind that while FD has to be active when the attack connects, any extra time before/after is wasted Poise. Ideally players should press the buttons briefly - slightly longer than a tap - just before the attacker's hit connects. With some practice, defenders can save some Poise by tapping Faultless Guard right before each hit of an attacker's blockstring!

Instant Block

Guard right before an attack connects (small window)

Instant Blocking is a more powerful, advanced form of blocking. On successful Instant Block, Mistiming an Instant Block locks out IBing subsequent attempts until a brief period of not blocking. Holding block counts towards this time.

Instant Blocking has the benefit of:

  • Prevents chip damage
  • Pushback for the defender is reduced to zero
  • Gain twice the amount of Piose on block
  • Instant blocking a strike in the air reduces the landing recovery duration.

IB does not allow you to make "illegal" blocks: you still must guard high/low correctly.

While blocking, players can attempt an Instant Block at any point. Think of it as a minigame: time your guard to the rhythm of the opponent's hits.

Since faster attacks generally have shorter range, Instant Blocking an opponent's attack often creates new punish opportunities. Instant blocking might even be required to punish certain attacks that are at frame disadvantage but cause too much pushback to punish under normal circumstances.

Guarded Attacks

Performed with a light attack while holding block and in blockstun.

Guarded Attacks are Hitbox protected attacks that can be done during ground blockstun, making them useful to interrupt an attacking opponent and reset the momentum of the match. These are performed at the cost of Poise (2-3 blocks?) They must hit an opponent or they whiff. Activating clears blockstun and exits guard but has it’s own recovery and attacks can only link not cancel from it, limiting follow up making it an attack based alternative to Yellow roman cancel with higher risk/reward.

Shove

Blocking is a core part of the classic Rock, paper, Scissors game that Fighting games are typically described as at their core. Typically attacks are beaten by blocking, blocking is beaten by throws (grabs in this game, and possibly Heavy Control attacks) and throws are beaten by attacks. Fighting games ultimately end up more nuanced than this but this is the core concept. However Typically only attacks and throws result in a state where the player that wins gains an advantage. Blocking generally returns players to neutral putting it in a bit of a different category, it doesn’t really “beat” attacks it just reduces their effectiveness. Further sub elements within attacks like High, mid and low attack spaces, add an additional layer to attacks that defenders must keep up with to break even. Because it doesn’t really make sense to consider defending as a form of attack defense is then often given a lot of additional options.

That said this is not to discount that blocking and it’s cousins in the defensive space don’t also exist in balance with the opponent's ability to string attacks together in a way that leaves no openings for the blocking player to turn things around and that punishes in and of themselves represent this.

This game also has an expanded defensive space to hold off attackers such as, blocks, faultless defense, dodges/dashes/rolls, parries, combo breakers, and deflects/reflects. With parries, combo breakers, and reflects have the most potential to create opportunities to turn defense into offense. To expand this defensive space further there is the Shove.

A shove is a move available as a result of considering Block as a charged move. Essentially this adds a potential motion control aspect to the block that creates a sort of Blocking “special”. Like other specials this requires a negative edge motion input first holding block and then performing a back then forward movement while releasing the block key at the end. This executes the shove, which can whiff if it misses or it can be blocked and is still vulnerable to grabs. This allows it to be baited but if it makes contact with an attacking opponent the recovery can be cancelled if it is not blocked. If the Shoves “hit” lands/isn’t blocked, then a hitstun is applied to the opponent and leads to an opening for an attack or grab.

This is the closest equivalent to combo-breaking but from the blocking position and unlike combo break puts the player at an advantage.

Parry/Deflect/Reflect

Parries, are performed by releasing a block within a small window at the end of the startup of an opponent's attack opening the opponent up by forcing the opponent into a whiffed state. The player cannot already be in block stun or hit stun to parry.

A successful Parry cancels any block recovery and the recovery animation from Parry can be canceled by any move.

This is more powerful than instant block but takes the player out of block after it’s timing window making it riskier. Because of the risk this carries it is more rewarding:

  • Prevents chip damage
  • Pushback for the defender is reduced to zero
  • add recovery frames to the opponent
  • Gain additional poise.
  • Parrying a strike in the air reduces the landing recovery duration.

Parries only need to be within range of the attacks hurtbox, not the opponent's hitboxes

Deflect

Is the same as a parry but performed on projectiles, the projectile is deflected away from the player.

Reflect

Same as deflect but requires the player to face the opponent who fires the projectile. Returns the projectile back at them.

If these actions affect an opponent, they apply to all attacks within their animation window (multiple opponents attacks)

Additional considerations:

It may be useful to consider parry deflect and reflect as directional if possible. This may make sense in the context of releasing the guard/guard state button. But the idea would be that this could help with managing incoming ranged/projectile attacks from various direction and not restrict it to the forward facing space. This adjustment may have other knock ons that would need to also be considered. Another option may be to keep the guard state directional as well but again this could have other knock on’s. additional consideration should be made regarding this and discussed further as well as be considered a major testing element in prototyping.
