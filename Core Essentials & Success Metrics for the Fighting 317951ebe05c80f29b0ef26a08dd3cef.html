Core Essentials & Success Metrics for the Fighting Game

Purpose

While the long-term goal is to build a connected ecosystem (Fighting Game → MOBA → MMORPG), the fighting game must first stand on its own as a complete, satisfying experience. This section defines the core essentials that modern players expect from a fighting game in 2026, as well as the success metrics we will use to measure whether the game is achieving its goals.This is not an attempt to make a traditional 2D fighter — it is a deliberate 3rd-person high-fantasy RPG-hybrid. However, we must still meet or exceed industry standards in the areas that matter most to players, especially around fairness, accessibility, and long-term engagement.Core Essentials (Must-Haves)These are the non-negotiable features that define a modern fighting game experience. We will prioritize them from the very beginning:

  1. Netcode & Online Play
    • Rollback netcode as the foundation (Mover 2.0 in UE 5.7).
    • Low-latency matchmaking with good region support.
    • Spectator mode and replay system from day one.
    • Future-proofing for larger player counts (preparing for MOBA scaling).
  1. Practice & Training Tools
    • Robust training mode with frame data display, hitbox visualization, dummy recording, and combo practice.
    • Clear, accessible tutorials that teach the unique 3rd-person control scheme and camera behavior.
    • Replay analysis tools that highlight Yomi decisions and resource management.
  1. Matchmaking & Progression
    • Skill-based matchmaking that feels fair for both new and experienced players.
    • Clear progression systems (ranks, cosmetics, skill unlocks) that reward time investment without pay-to-win.
    • Strong onboarding flow that makes the 3rd-person camera and state-based controls feel approachable.
  1. Readability & Accessibility
    • Excellent visual feedback (hit flashes, directional indicators, environmental transparency).
    • Customizable camera options (FOV, distance, shake toggle).
    • Color-blind support, input buffering, and accessibility settings.
  1. Community & Longevity Features
    • Local multiplayer support.
    • Mod support or workshop tools (especially important for skill customization).
    • Active developer communication and regular balance patches.

Success Metrics

We will measure success at the fighting game stage by these targets:Short-Term (First 6–12 Months)

  • Positive player feedback on combat feel and camera readability (Steam reviews ≥ 80% Positive).
  • Stable player base with good retention (daily/weekly active users).
  • Strong netcode performance (average rollback < 3 frames in most matches).

Medium-Term (Year 1–2)

  • Healthy competitive scene (tournaments, content creators, community events).
  • Skill customization system praised for depth and creativity.
  • Successful transition of players from fighting game to MOBA testing.

Long-Term

  • The fighting game generates enough revenue and goodwill to fund MOBA development.
  • Core mechanics prove scalable (no major rework needed when expanding to MOBA/MMORPG).

Note on Genre Knowledge

The developer’s background is not in traditional fighting games, but in high-fantasy action RPGs. This is viewed as a strength: the goal is not to replicate Street Fighter or Tekken exactly, but to use fighting game principles (Yomi, resource tension, precise timing) as an elegant way to deliver deep, expressive combat that scales beautifully into larger experiences.
