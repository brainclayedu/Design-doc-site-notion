Combat States

Base state
Impact State

Goals

The Impact State mechanic is designed to:

  • Expand Tactical Options: Provide aimed, disruptive attacks (knockup, knockdown, control) to shift combat momentum.
  • Enhance Precision Play: Leverage the 3rd-person aimed state for targeted, high-impact moves, balancing risk with reward.
  • Integrate RPG Depth: Introduce crowd control skills distinct from grappling, enriching strategic variety.
  • Support Resource Dynamics: Tie actions to Burst and skill-specific costs, reinforcing "Core Loop" tension.

Overview

Impact State activates by holding the Heavy Attack button (RT/RMB), shifting the player into an aimed state with a tight, over-the-shoulder camera and slowed movement for precision targeting. This state modifies two inputs: Light Attack becomes a knockup (low/mid) or knockdown (overhead) tool based on attack space, and Grab becomes the Control Skill button, applying crowd control effects (e.g., Stun, Root) inspired by RPG mechanics. A third option, Wild Assault (Impact + Dash), consumes Burst for a forward-rushing assault, broadening Impact’s disruptive potential. Releasing RT without using these options triggers a charged, directional Heavy Attack from Base State, while Dodge/Evade and Jump remain camera-relative for mobility. Distinct from "Grabs" and "Grappling State Skills," Impact State focuses on standalone disruption—knockups set up air combos, knockdowns create prone openings, and Control Skills limit foe actions—complementing the rock-paper-scissors counterplay of "Core Loop"’s Prediction and Advantage phases. Visual feedback (e.g., HUD tags like "KNOCKUP," "CONTROL") and subtle animations (e.g., a windup for Wild Assault) ensure clarity in The Fighting Game’s high-fantasy chaos.

Key Features

  • State Activation: Hold RT/RMB to enter Impact State—aimed camera tightens, movement slows for precision; releasing unused reverts to a charged Base State Heavy Attack (directional).
  • Knockup/Knockdown Attack: Light Attack (RB/LMB) shifts to aimed knockup (low/mid attack space) or knockdown (overhead attack space), disrupting foes—e.g., knockup launches for air follow-ups, knockdown forces prone recovery.
  • Control Skill: Grab (LT/ALT) becomes an aimed Control Skill, applying RPG-style crowd control (e.g., ~1-2 second Stun, Root), distinct from grappling—costs vary by skill (e.g., Energy, Poise).
  • Wild Assault: Dash (B/MMB) in Impact State triggers a Burst-fueled rush (~half Burst gauge), charging forward to knock foes back—high-risk, high-reward disruption.
  • Mobility Retention: Dodge/Evade and Jump remain directional (camera-relative), offering escape or repositioning within the aimed state.
  • Feedback Signals: HUD tags ("KNOCKUP," "KNOCKDOWN," "CONTROL," "WILD") and animations (e.g., upward swing for knockup, lunge for Wild Assault) clarify actions.

Challenges and Solutions

  • Attack Space Ambiguity: Aimed reticle (high = knockdown, low/mid = knockup) vs. move design (e.g., overheads locked as knockdown) risks player confusion—e.g., aiming a low overhead misfires.
    • Solution: Test reticle-driven space (player aim defines outcome) vs. fixed animations (move type locks effect)—e.g., projectiles follow aim, strikes stay consistent—balancing control and predictability.
  • Resource Overlap: Wild Assault (Burst) and Control Skill (Energy/Poise) lack unified costs, risking imbalance—e.g., spammable knockups vs. rare Wild Assaults.
    • Solution: Defer exact costs to skill crafting—prototype with ~5-10% Energy for knockup/knockdown, ~10-20% Energy + 1-2 Poise for Control Skill, ~50% Burst for Wild Assault—ensuring tactical trade-offs.
  • State Overlap: Knockup/knockdown echoes Pre-Jump, Control Skill mimics "Grabs," potentially diluting Impact’s niche.
    • Solution: Differentiate via intent—Pre-Jump for reversals (~5f invuln), Impact for broad disruption (e.g., hard knockdowns); Control Skill as crowd control, not grapple entry—refining in testing.
  • Input Clarity: RT hold for state vs. release for charged attack might confuse timing—e.g., accidental Base State triggers.
    • Solution: Add a ~5-10f buffer for Impact actions post-RT hold, reducing misfires—visual glow signals state entry.

Suggestions for Prototyping

  • Attack Space Testing: Experiment with reticle aim (high = knockdown, low/mid = knockup) vs. fixed move types—e.g., overheads always knockdown (~15f startup), projectiles shift with aim—tracking player feedback on control vs. consistency.
  • Resource Balancing: Test knockup/knockdown at ~5-10% Energy, Control Skill at ~10-20% Energy + 1-2 Poise, Wild Assault at ~50% Burst—adjust based on frequency and impact (e.g., ~1-2 uses per exchange).
  • Animation Variety: Trial distinct knockup (e.g., 10f startup, soft launch) vs. knockdown (e.g., ~15f startup, hard prone) timings, ensuring Control Skill (1-2s effects) feels potent yet interruptible.
  • Wild Assault Range: Test forward rush distance (3-5m) and knockback (2-3m), balancing Burst cost with multi-foe potential—e.g., hits ~2 targets in group scenarios.

Conclusion

Impact State enriches The Fighting Game’s combat with aimed, disruptive tools—knockup/knockdown attacks, Control Skills, and Wild Assault—blending fighting game precision with RPG crowd control. Activated via RT hold, it shifts Light Attack and Grab into tactical options, with Burst-driven Wild Assault adding a high-stakes rush, all distinct from "Grabs" grappling flow. Tied to the "Core Loop"’s Prediction-to-Advantage shift, it rewards timing and positioning, with HUD tags and animations ensuring readability. Prototyping will refine attack space logic, resource costs, and differentiation from Pre-Jump, solidifying Impact State as a versatile, scalable mechanic in this high-fantasy arena.

Focus State

This refers to an attack done from the focus state that is active while holding the grab button. This is the main aimed attack state and can apply an additional set of both aimed light and heavy attacks.

This is currently considered the primary category for proximity normals where an attack can have a close and far variant but this may not make sense to be limited to this attack category

Guard state

Guarding against attacks, commonly known as blocking, is to keep yourself alive.

There are many different types of defense, and each is useful for different situations. Correctly picking the right one for each situation is critical for survivability.

Guard (block)

When a player defends an attack using a Guard, they are put into a state called blockstun during which their ability to act is mostly taken away. Yellow Roman Cancel, Blue Burst, and Deflect Shield can still be used during blockstun.

Pushback

Blocking an attack also pushes the defender back. This phenomenon is called pushback. The amount of pushback varies by attack, though generally the higher the Attack Level, the more pushback. Some attacks even pull in the defender instead of pushing them back! When the defender blocks a strike in a corner, pushback is applied to the attacker instead, making sure that the net pushback both characters experience is the same regardless of positioning.

Some attacks like projectiles or beams performed at a significant range may not cause pushback. Incorporating ranges combat more holistically into the combat mechanics may require some additional elements not generally found in fighting games where combat is broadly more melee-focused at some level.

Unblockable Attack

Some attacks cannot be guarded against. These generally fall into two categories: Grabs, and Controls. Grabs are proximity-based attacks that cannot be guarded against. Controls are similar to grab but are focused on crowd control that puts the opponent into typically a hard CC.

Grounded Guard

A basic grounded guard is the most common defensive option. Also known as blocking.

There are 2 types of grounded guards: standing guard and crouching guard. Standing guard will block high and mid attacks, crouching guard will block mid and low attacks.

From prone this depends on the orientation of the player (stomach or back = head only or Upper and lower body) but the opponent's ability to hit the vulnerable hitbox is dependent on their positioning as well.

Standing vs. Crouching Guard

Standing guard experiences more pushback than crouching guard. This means that strategically using standing guard during an attacker's blockstring can push the defender out of range sooner and potentially enable a punish, especially if the additional pushback makes an attack whiff unexpectedly.

On the other hand, the crouching guard can be used to stay closer to the attacker and let the defender use their faster, shorter-range buttons at an opportune time.

Airborne Guard

There is no high-low guard in the air, making it a common place for defenders to go to avoid blocking ground-based high/low/throw mix-up.

That's not to say that being airborne is a fail-safe defensive posture; getting airborne is a risk since jumps have a bit of vulnerable start-up, and blocking a strike will force the defender into blockstun until landing, plus additional grounded blockstun. Landing blockstun is reduced on air Instant Block or air Instant Faultless Defense. Air blocking certain attacks push the defender higher into the air, effectively giving the attacker more frame advantage.

Air-blocking projectiles will not force defenders into blockstun until landing, but if they do land, they will be forced into grounded blockstun. If the defender recovers in the air, then air options are restored.
