Aerial Movement

Aerial movement shapes how characters take flight in The Fighting Game, weaving a dynamic vertical dimension into its high-fantasy, 3rd-person combat. This system empowers players to leap, strike, and maneuver mid-air, enhancing offense, evasion, and positioning across vibrant arenas. Anchored by jumping and double jumping, it’s enriched with pre-jump charging, air aiming precision, swift dashes, and recovery mechanics—all governed by Energy costs and character builds. Aerial movement elevates the battlefield, offering a fluid, strategic layer that complements grounded play.

Goals

The aerial movement system strives to:

  • Broaden Combat Depth: Enable aerial attacks, evasions, and positioning, expanding tactical options beyond the ground plane.
  • Create a fast-paced combat layer: The aerial movement is generally faster-paced than the walking-centric ground combat, necessitated by the speed of available options, air dashes, and jumps.
  • Reflect Build Diversity: Adjust jump height, air mobility, and recovery to suit agile, sturdy, ranged, or aerial-focused playstyles.
  • Balance Risk and Reward: Provide aerial advantages—like evasion or surprise—offset by vulnerabilities such as landing recovery, ensuring strategic interplay.
  • Ensure Fluidity: Integrate seamlessly with grounded movement and combat, maintaining an intuitive, natural flow.

Overview

Aerial movement begins with jumping, the gateway to the skies, followed by double jumping for extended airtime—both enabling offense, defense, and traversal. Pre-jump charges up potent knockup strikes, air aiming sharpens mid-flight precision for ranged and melee alike, and air dashes or evades offer quick bursts of mobility. Regaining air options sustains aerial agility, while landing recovery introduces vulnerability. Rooted in Energy, stats (e.g., agility, strength), and gear weight, this system rewards skillful execution and reflects a player’s unique combat style.

Core Mechanics

Jumping

Jumping launches characters into the air, serving as the bedrock of aerial movement. Its versatility draws from Super Smash Bros.’s leap mechanics, adapted for a 3D fantasy brawler.

  • Mechanics: A quick tap triggers a short hop—enough to clear low sweeps—while holding scales to a fuller leap, capped by stats and gear (agile builds jump higher, heavy ones less). It costs Energy—minimal for hops, slightly more for full jumps—blending RPG scaling with fighting game finesse.
  • Combat Role: Jumps evade ground attacks—like a low kick—or set up aerial strikes, such as a diving slash. In the Directional State, it’s omnidirectional; in the Aimed State, it aligns with the reticle.
  • Tactical Use: Players leap over hazards, close gaps, or reach platforms—like in Assassin’s Creed—balanced by Energy use and landing risks.

Double Jumping

Double jumping extends aerial presence, allowing a second leap mid-air, echoing Super Smash Bros.’s multi-jump archetype for prolonged airtime and flexibility.

  • Mechanics: Triggered by a second jump input after the first, it matches the initial jump’s height (agile builds soar farther, heavy ones less), costing additional Energy—slightly higher than the first jump. Agility boosts distance; weight curbs it.
  • Combat Role: Enables chaining aerial attacks—like a second kick after a hop—or evading mid-air threats, similar to Marvel vs. Capcom’s air combos. It flows into air aiming or dashes effortlessly.
  • Tactical Use: Players dodge projectiles with a double jump or reposition for a surprise strike—e.g., leaping twice to flank a foe—offering aerial-focused builds a potent edge, balanced by Energy drain.

Pre-Jump

Pre-jump is a charging window before takeoff, activated by holding the jump input. It’s a setup phase—not an attack itself—enabling powerful, blockable jumping attacks like a classic Dragon Punch from Street Fighter.

  • Mechanics: Lasts a brief duration (e.g., a short window as an example), unblockable as a state but throwable, with agility speeding it up and weight slowing it. It’s not an attack—light or heavy inputs during pre-jump trigger jumping attacks that cost extra Energy and can be blocked mid-air.
  • Combat Role: Jumping attacks launched from pre-jump—like a rising uppercut—knock foes upward for combos, distinct from Guilty Gear’s pre-jump frames by focusing on setup rather than immediate strikes. Charged versions amplify damage or height.
  • Tactical Use: It’s a high-risk opener—e.g., catching a landing foe—or evasion tool, offset by vulnerability to grabs, akin to wrestling’s pre-move tension.

Air Aiming

Air aiming refines mid-flight precision, activated by holding an attack input while airborne, briefly slowing the jump arc for both ranged and melee combat.

  • Mechanics: Slows arc speed temporarily (e.g., a noticeable reduction), reverting naturally, with no extra Energy cost beyond the jump. Agility extends this window; heavier builds shorten it. Attacks lock to the soft-lock reticle in the Aimed State.
  • Combat Role: Enables ranged skillshots—like a mid-air fireball—or melee precision—like a diving sword thrust—blending Dragon Ball Z: Budokai’s blasts with Devil May Cry’s aerial slashes, suiting diverse builds.
  • Tactical Use: Players target foes with ranged bolts or plunge into melee, trading mobility for accuracy—e.g., aiming a spear at a dodging opponent—but risk exposure to counterattacks.

Air Dash/Evade

Air dash/evade delivers a swift mid-air burst, triggered by a dash input while airborne, offering evasion or repositioning, inspired by Guilty Gear’s air dashes with a one-use limit per jump. This is the only positional tool available in the air (unless the character has traversal options like flight or a grappling hook)

  • Mechanics: Moves in the chosen direction (relative to camera), costing Energy—agile builds dash farther, heavy ones less. Speed and startup vary by build—light characters dash quicker—with a single use per jump cycle.
  • Combat Role: Dodges aerial strikes—like evading a projectile—or repositions for attacks. It pairs with aimed strikes seamlessly.
  • Tactical Use: Shifts position for flanking—e.g., dashing to a foe’s side—or escapes projectiles, balanced by Energy cost and its once-per-jump limit.

Regaining Air Options

Characters refresh aerial capabilities (jumps, dashes) through specific actions, sustaining air play.

  • Mechanics: Resets occur by landing, air-blocking or parrying a projectile (exiting stun), or escaping an air throw—no extra Energy beyond the action itself.
  • Combat Role: Allows a follow-up jump or dash—e.g., parrying a bolt to double jump—extending combos like Marvel vs. Capcom’s air chains.
  • Tactical Use: Rewards skill—e.g., parrying to leap higher—while landing resets provide recovery, balancing aerial freedom with grounded play.

Landing Recovery

Landing imposes a brief recovery period post-air, adding vulnerability.

  • Mechanics: Empty jumps restrict movement briefly (e.g., a short delay), still allowing attacks; landings after actions (e.g., dashes, strikes) enforce a fuller recovery (e.g., slightly longer, no actions)—agility shortens both, strength reduces stun.
  • Combat Role: Punishes sloppy air play—like a missed dive leaving an opening—yet permits counters if timed, akin to Smash Bros.’s landing dynamics.
  • Tactical Use: Demands smart landings—e.g., aiming for safety—or risks exposure, balanced by build resilience.

Integration with Combat and Movement

Aerial movement enhances the game’s flow:

  • Combat States: In the Directional State, jumps and dashes are omnidirectional, evading ground threats; in the Aimed State, they sharpen for precision—like a mid-air thrust.
  • Energy Dynamics: Jumps, double jumps, and dashes draw Energy—pre-jump attacks add more—preserving grounded options unless committed aloft.
  • Grounded Ties: Pre-jump flows from walking or crouching, double jumps extend dashes, linking aerial to grounded seamlessly.

Build Customization

Character builds shape aerial performance:

  • Stats: Agility boosts jump height and dash distance; strength cuts landing recovery—e.g., sturdy builds recover faster.
  • Gear Weight: Light armor enhances air speed; heavy armor limits height but bolsters resilience—e.g., less knockback.
  • Class Skills: Aerial builds (e.g., flyers) extend jump time; grapplers widen air grab range—e.g., snaring a foe mid-leap.

Challenges and Solutions

Aerial movement presents design hurdles:

  • Precision Difficulty: Air aiming might feel clunky on gamepads for ranged or melee.
    • Solution: Strengthen the soft-lock reticle and buffer inputs—ensuring seamless strikes, whether a fireball or a slash.
  • Landing Vulnerability: Recovery could overly punish aerial aggression.
    • Solution: Shorten recovery for agile builds and boost strength resilience—balancing risk with viability.
  • Balance Across Builds: Aerial-focused builds might overshadow ground ones.
    • Solution: Tweak Energy costs and stat scaling—e.g., heavy builds pay more for jumps—ensuring ground styles remain competitive.

Conclusion

Aerial movement adds a soaring dimension to The Fighting Game, blending jumping and double jumping’s versatility, pre-jump’s charged potency, and air dashes’ agility into a unified system. Rooted in Energy, stats, and combat flow, it empowers players to strike from above, dodge mid-air, or recover strategically—elevating the game’s depth with every leap and landing.
