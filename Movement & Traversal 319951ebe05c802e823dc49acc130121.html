Movement & Traversal

Introduction

Movement in this game should feel fluid, responsive, and deeply tactical. Every dash, sprint, and stance shift has real weight and consequence. You feel the Energy drain as you close the distance or evade a heavy swing, and the satisfaction of a perfectly timed dash that resets the fight on your terms. Movement isn’t just getting from point A to B — it’s a core part of the Yomi loop, letting you control space, bait commitments, and set up your next big read in a cinematic over-the-shoulder view.

Movement defines how players navigate the arenas of this combat experience, blending fluid traversal with tactical depth to enhance both duels and broader engagements. Viewed through an over-the-shoulder perspective, the system ensures every step, dash, or jump feels responsive and purposeful, supporting strategic positioning while tying into combat and character customization. It balances agility with deliberation, offering a range of options from quiet walks to swift sprints, all shaped by a player’s build and resource management.

Goals

The movement system is crafted to:

  • Feel Fluid: Seamlessly carry momentum into attacks, allowing a dodge to flow into a strike or a sprint to transition into a powerful blow.
  • Stay Responsive: Deliver quick, intuitive control for reacting to threats and repositioning, optimized for gamepad accessibility.
  • Enable Positioning: Provide tactical choices—evading into cover, leaping for height, or crouching for stealth—to shape combat outcomes.
  • Support Combat Balance: Maintain a deliberate pace that complements the game’s fighting mechanics, preventing excessive speed from outpacing reactions.
  • Reflect Customization: Adjust movement through character stats, gear weight, and class skills, ensuring diverse playstyles with clear trade-offs.

Overview

At its heart, movement is an 8-way directional system rooted in a deliberate walk—faster than a stroll, slower than a jog—prioritizing precision over haste. This walk speed varies with a character’s build: nimble fighters move quicker, while those in heavy armor tread slower, reflecting their resilience. Two core combat states define the system:

  • Directional State: A wider camera view offers broad awareness, with strafing and backpedaling for adaptable, omnidirectional movement.
  • Aimed State: A tighter, close-up view slows mobility for precision, focusing on aimed attacks and skillshots.

These states anchor a suite of movement options—walking, sprinting, dashing, jumping, crouching, proning, and traversal—each tied to Energy costs that scale with gear and stats. Beyond combat, movement extends to environmental interactions like climbing or vaulting, turning arenas into dynamic spaces for tactical advantage. This creates a system where every action, from a quiet step to a charged leap, enhances both strategy and immersion.

Core Movement States

Directional State

The camera pulls back slightly, offset to the right, providing a wide view to assess threats and surroundings—perfect for spotting an ambush or navigating hazards. Players strafe and backpedal instead of turning to face their movement direction, with only minor slowdowns to keep responsiveness intact. This deliberate pace lets players sidestep melee strikes or circle foes, maintaining awareness in chaotic duels without constant camera adjustments. Attacks in this state are either forward-facing or applied in an omnidirectional way, guided by movement inputs, making it ideal for close-quarters adaptability.

Aimed State

The camera closes in over the shoulder, emphasizing precision for targeting opponents or environmental points. Movement slows to deliberate, even steps with a brief acceleration from neutral, trading speed for accuracy. Special attacks here use motion inputs—such as back-then-forward or quarter-circle movements—triggered by holding and releasing an attack button within a brief window, adding depth to aimed strikes or retreats. This state suits players landing a pinpoint projectile or setting up a charged blow, balancing risk with reward through focused control.

Movement and Integration

The system encompasses a variety of movement options, each enhancing combat and traversal:

  • Walking: The default 8-way stance offers precise, Energy-neutral control, quiet enough to avoid detection. Speed adjusts with agility and gear weight—light builds glide faster, heavy ones step with purpose.
  • Sprinting: A faster gait drains Energy continuously, ideal for closing gaps or fleeing, but slows under attack. It shifts to a state with momentum-driven attacks, like a charging strike, balancing speed with vulnerability.
  • Dashing/Dodging: Quick bursts cost Energy, scaled by build—agile characters cover more ground, heavy ones less. These evade strikes or adjust aim, offering reactive flexibility in both states.
  • Jumping: Leaps scale with hold duration, entering a charged state for aerial knockup attacks. Energy costs reflect height and weight, adding verticality to tactics.
  • Crouching: Slows movement, shrinks the hitbox, and enables low attacks or stealth, costing no Energy—perfect for dodging high blows or sneaking into position.
  • Proning: Drops to the ground, limiting mobility but altering vulnerability for defense or low strikes—useful after a knockdown or as a tactical choice.
  • Traversal: Climbing and vaulting scale walls or leap obstacles, costing Energy based on agility and weight. These turn arenas into multi-level battlegrounds for ambushes or escapes.
  • Stealth: A utility option from crouching or proning reduces detection—quiet steps or class skills like invisibility—balanced by Energy and duration limits.
Movement Types

Movement types define how characters navigate both combat and the broader arena in The Fighting Game, serving as a vital component of its tactical gameplay and character identity. These mechanics fuse the precision of fighting games with the flexibility of RPGs, offering players diverse ways to express their playstyle within a 3rd-person over-the-shoulder perspective. Movement is categorized into two key areas: Combat movement, which shapes combat movement, and traversal, which governs broader arena mobility. Together, these create a dynamic system where every step, dodge, or leap influences the battlefield experience.

Goals

The movement system is crafted to:

  • Enhance Character Diversity: Ensure each character’s movement reflects a unique playstyle—aggressive, defensive, stealthy, or ranged—for distinct combat feels.
  • Provide Tactical Flexibility: Allow players to adapt movement to various scenarios, from dueling in tight spaces to navigating expansive arenas.
  • Integrate Customization: Tie movement to character builds, enabling players to tailor options that complement their combat approach.
  • Balance Complexity: Offer variety without overwhelming development or accessibility, starting with core mechanics and expanding thoughtfully over time.

Overview

Movement types split into two complementary categories:

  • Combat movement: determines the players movement options while in combat
  • Traversal: Manages movement across the arena, inspired by superhero games. Options like Parkour, flying, grappling hooks, or enhanced leaps can be combined to enable strategic repositioning and environmental mastery.

These categories work together: combat movement sets the rhythm of combat, while traversal expands the scope, turning arenas into multi-dimensional spaces for tactical play.

Combat Movement

Combat movement dictates most movement during combat, such as when the player is on the ground or in the air.

Grounded Movement

Grounded movement governs how characters navigate the arena floor in The Fighting Game, anchoring their combat presence and tactical options within its high-fantasy, 3rd-person battles. Designed to balance precision with versatility, this system encompasses walking, crouching, and proning—each offering unique ways to outmaneuver foes, evade threats, or seize battlefield advantages. Integrated with Energy costs, build customization, and combat states, grounded movement ensures every action is deliberate, rewarding skill and intent with tangible impact.

Goals

The system is built to:

  • Enable Precision: Deliver controlled movement for exact spacing, dodging threats, or preparing attacks with finesse.
  • Support Playstyle Variety: Cater to agile skirmishers, stealth operatives, grappling brawlers, or resilient tanks, avoiding sluggishness unless by design.
  • Enhance Combat: Allow effective evasion of close-range attacks, reserving faster maneuvers for broader challenges like projectiles or area effects.
  • Reflect Customization: Adjust speed, resilience, and utility through stats, gear weight, and class skills, creating meaningful trade-offs.

Overview

Grounded movement forms the core of a character’s arena presence, blending tactical flexibility with intentional pacing. Walking offers quiet, precise control, scaling with agility and gear weight to suit each build. Crouching trades speed for stealth and a reduced profile, enabling low strikes or sneaky approaches. Proning transforms the ground into a combat zone—ideal for grappling, ranged ambushes, or recovery—but remains vulnerable up close unless tailored for ground fighting.

Core Mechanics

Walking

Walking is the default grounded movement, an 8-way directional stance that prioritizes precision over haste—faster than a leisurely stroll but slower than a jog. It thrives in the Directional State, where the camera widens for awareness, letting players strafe or backpedal with subtle slowdowns.

  • Mechanics: Speed scales with agility (faster for nimble builds as an example) and gear weight (slower for heavier setups), costing no Energy and producing minimal noise—similar to quiet stalking in stealth games. Strafing and backpedaling incur a slight penalty, maintaining responsiveness without disrupting combat flow.
  • Combat Role: Players dodge melee swings through spacing—e.g., maintaining just beyond a sword’s reach—or circle foes for flanking, akin to circling in wrestling games but omnidirectional. It flows into attacks seamlessly, supporting both offense and defense.
  • Tactical Use: Its steady controlled steps drive clear positioning. It is the foundational movement in combat and It’s ideal for baiting—e.g., holding ground to provoke a miss—or managing multiple foes with broad awareness.
  • Comparison: Unlike the rapid jogs of action RPGs, walking mirrors the deliberate pace of fighting games, ensuring gamepad players can react to melee threats without feeling rushed. It avoids the speed of battle royale titles, focusing on controlled engagement.

Crouching

Crouching acts as a sub state that modifies attacks for the low attack space (e.g., Leg sweeps) and initially indicates a less drastic crouch before transitioning to a more full crouch, slowing movement significantly, shrinking the hitbox to evade high attacks and enabling stealth. It’s activated by a hold or press, staying Energy-neutral but reducing speed.

  • Mechanics: Speed drops noticeably—lighter build styles can lessen this penalty (e.g., slightly faster for light builds), while heavy gear exacerbates it (e.g., even slower)—reflecting a stealth crawl. It reduces noise further than walking and shifts vulnerability to low zones.
  • Combat Role: A crouch ducks overhead attacks, and enables specific low attacks. In the Aimed State, it adjusts aim for low skill-shots—e.g., a crouched arrow at a foe’s legs—while the Directional State supports stealthy strafing.
  • Tactical Use: It’s perfect for evasion—e.g., slipping under a heavy swing—or sneaking into cover, akin to stealth titles. It sets up ambushes, letting a rogue drop low and strike from hiding, balanced by its reduced pace against fast foes.
  • Comparison: Like stealth games’ crouch mechanics, it trades speed for subtlety and hitbox control, distinct from fast-paced shooters’ rapid crouching. It prioritizes evasion and patience over mobility, fitting the game’s deliberate tone.

Proning

Proning drops the character to the ground putting them in the Prone State—voluntarily or via knockdown—turning it into a tactical space for ground combat, stealth, or recovery, inspired by UFC and wrestling dynamics. It’s slow but versatile, with belly (facing forward) or back (facing up) orientations shifting via knockdowns or camera turns.

  • Mechanics: Speed reduces to a crawl—agility slightly improves this (e.g., faster for agile builds)—costing no Energy base, akin to a wrestler’s grounded shuffle. Belly proning moves forward/back via crawling, back proning shimmies forward/back or rolls side-to-side (short distances, Energy-free). Knockdowns dictate stance—front hits land on back, back hits on belly—each with unique movesets.
  • Combat Role:
    • Ground Fighting: Belly proning offers ranged attacks (e.g., a dagger throw, moderate Energy cost) or light grapples (e.g., a leg grab), while back proning enables blocks or submissions (e.g., a chokehold, or armbar). Grappling is single-target, lacking area damage unless manipulating the opponent —e.g., a choke pins one foe for a brief duration, applying slow—but exposes both grappler and target to friendly fire (e.g., an ally’s stray strike hits full force). Note grappling is not exclusive to the Prone state and is a part of the Grab mechanics.
    • Stealth and Ranged: Proning reduces visibility in cover (e.g., grass), letting a sniper hurl a low projectile (e.g., moderate Energy cost) with less exposure—similar to a hidden archer’s shot.
    • Vulnerability: Prone characters can end up in a more vulnerable or stronger position depending on how they are focused. A grappling setup may be dangerous up close but vulnerable to even mid-range if their attacks simply have less reach than their standing opponent. Inversely, a prone player with more range focus could find themselves vulnerable to a standing opponent on top of them in melee. Friendly fire risks escalate in grapples and prone, impacting both parties.
  • Tactical Use: Proning baits foes into ground exchanges—e.g., a wrestler pins for a choke—or hides for ambushes. Post-knockdown, it turns falls into counter opportunities, balanced by melee risk unless built with some grappling focus.
  • Comparison: Like UFC’s ground-and-pound and submission hold, proning offers grappling and strikes but stays vulnerable to standing foes, unlike rapid recovery in action RPGs. It’s slower than shooters’ slides but richer in combat options, echoing wrestling’s positional tension.

Integration with Combat and Movement

Grounded movement blends with the game’s systems:

  • Combat States: Walking strafes in the Directional State for melee evasion, while crouching and proning adjust aim in the Aimed State—e.g., a prone sniper shot or crouched low thrust.
  • Energy Dynamics: Walking and crouching cost no Energy, preserving it for dashes or grapples; prone actions (e.g., submissions) use Energy (e.g., light to moderate costs) or Poise (e.g., 1 block), scaled by build efficiency.
  • Traversal Link: Walking flows into sprinting, crouching sets up stealth, and proning rolls into dashes—connecting grounded to broader mobility.

Build Customization

Character builds tailor grounded movement:

  • Stats: Could affect speeds (e.g., faster walking, quicker prone crawl); Others could boost prone resilience and grapple power (e.g., stronger submissions).
  • Gear Weight: Lighter armor enhances speed (e.g., faster walking); heavy or more restrictive armor slows (e.g., reduced pace) but fortifies—e.g., less damage.
  • Class Skills: Stealth builds quieten crouching/proning (e.g., less noise); grapplers add prone submissions (e.g., an armbar that debuffs the opponent's attacks for a duration)—crafting unique profiles.

Challenges and Solutions

Grounded movement faces design hurdles:

  • Walking Pace Balance: Too slow dulls agility, too fast disrupts pacing.
    • Solution: Adjust speed with agility (e.g., light builds slightly faster)—offset by resilience perks for heavy builds, ensuring fairness.
  • Crouching Overuse: Excessive crouching could dodge too easily.
    • Solution: Cap speed (e.g., notably slow) and tie low attacks to Energy (e.g., small cost per strike), keeping it situational.
  • Proning Disconnect: Slow prone combat might feel passive or safe.
    • Solution: Add melee vulnerability (e.g., extra damage unless grappling-built) and enrich prone moves (e.g., grapples, ranged shots), ensuring active risk-reward.

Conclusion

Grounded movement crafts a precise, versatile framework for arena combat, blending walking’s control, crouching’s subtlety, and proning’s ground-game depth. Tied to Energy, stats, and combat flow, it empowers players to dodge, strike, or grapple from the floor—whether weaving through melee, ambushing from stealth, or wrestling back control. This system ensures every grounded action enriches the game’s tactical tapestry, offering a personalized battlefield experience.

Aerial Movement

Aerial movement shapes how characters take flight in The Fighting Game, weaving a dynamic vertical dimension into its high-fantasy, 3rd-person combat. This system empowers players to leap, strike, and maneuver mid-air, enhancing offense, evasion, and positioning across vibrant arenas. Anchored by jumping and double jumping, it’s enriched with pre-jump charging, air aiming precision, swift dashes, and recovery mechanics—all governed by Energy costs and character builds. Aerial movement elevates the battlefield, offering a fluid, strategic layer that complements grounded play.

Goals

The aerial movement system strives to:

  • Broaden Combat Depth: Enable aerial attacks, evasions, and positioning, expanding tactical options beyond the ground plane.
  • Create a fast-paced combat layer: The aerial movement is generally faster-paced than the walking-centric ground combat, necessitated by the speed of available options, air dashes, and jumps.
  • Reflect Build Diversity: Adjust jump height, air mobility, and recovery to suit agile, sturdy, ranged, or aerial-focused playstyles.
  • Balance Risk and Reward: Provide aerial advantages—like evasion or surprise—offset by vulnerabilities such as landing recovery, ensuring strategic interplay.
  • Ensure Fluidity: Integrate seamlessly with grounded movement and combat, maintaining an intuitive, natural flow.

Overview

Aerial movement begins with jumping, the gateway to the skies, followed by double jumping for extended airtime—both enabling offense, defense, and traversal. Pre-jump charges up potent knockup strikes, air aiming sharpens mid-flight precision for ranged and melee alike, and air dashes or evades offer quick bursts of mobility. Regaining air options sustains aerial agility, while landing recovery introduces vulnerability. Rooted in Energy, stats (e.g., agility, strength), and gear weight, this system rewards skillful execution and reflects a player’s unique combat style.

Core Mechanics

Jumping

Jumping launches characters into the air, serving as the bedrock of aerial movement. Its versatility draws from Super Smash Bros.’s leap mechanics, adapted for a 3D fantasy brawler.

  • Mechanics: A quick tap triggers a short hop—enough to clear low sweeps—while holding scales to a fuller leap, capped by stats and gear (agile builds jump higher, heavy ones less). It costs Energy—minimal for hops, slightly more for full jumps—blending RPG scaling with fighting game finesse.
  • Combat Role: Jumps evade ground attacks—like a low kick—or set up aerial strikes, such as a diving slash. In the Directional State, it’s omnidirectional; in the Aimed State, it aligns with the reticle.
  • Tactical Use: Players leap over hazards, close gaps, or reach platforms—like in Assassin’s Creed—balanced by Energy use and landing risks.

Double Jumping

Double jumping extends aerial presence, allowing a second leap mid-air, echoing Super Smash Bros.’s multi-jump archetype for prolonged airtime and flexibility.

  • Mechanics: Triggered by a second jump input after the first, it matches the initial jump’s height (agile builds soar farther, heavy ones less), costing additional Energy—slightly higher than the first jump. Agility boosts distance; weight curbs it.
  • Combat Role: Enables chaining aerial attacks—like a second kick after a hop—or evading mid-air threats, similar to Marvel vs. Capcom’s air combos. It flows into air aiming or dashes effortlessly.
  • Tactical Use: Players dodge projectiles with a double jump or reposition for a surprise strike—e.g., leaping twice to flank a foe—offering aerial-focused builds a potent edge, balanced by Energy drain.

Pre-Jump

Pre-jump is a charging window before takeoff, activated by holding the jump input. It’s a setup phase—not an attack itself—enabling powerful, blockable jumping attacks like a classic Dragon Punch from Street Fighter.

  • Mechanics: Lasts a brief duration (e.g., a short window as an example), unblockable as a state but throwable, with agility speeding it up and weight slowing it. It’s not an attack—light or heavy inputs during pre-jump trigger jumping attacks that cost extra Energy and can be blocked mid-air.
  • Combat Role: Jumping attacks launched from pre-jump—like a rising uppercut—knock foes upward for combos, distinct from Guilty Gear’s pre-jump frames by focusing on setup rather than immediate strikes. Charged versions amplify damage or height.
  • Tactical Use: It’s a high-risk opener—e.g., catching a landing foe—or evasion tool, offset by vulnerability to grabs, akin to wrestling’s pre-move tension.

Air Aiming

Air aiming refines mid-flight precision, activated by holding an attack input while airborne, briefly slowing the jump arc for both ranged and melee combat.

  • Mechanics: Slows arc speed temporarily (e.g., a noticeable reduction), reverting naturally, with no extra Energy cost beyond the jump. Agility extends this window; heavier builds shorten it. Attacks lock to the soft-lock reticle in the Aimed State.
  • Combat Role: Enables ranged skillshots—like a mid-air fireball—or melee precision—like a diving sword thrust—blending Dragon Ball Z: Budokai’s blasts with Devil May Cry’s aerial slashes, suiting diverse builds.
  • Tactical Use: Players target foes with ranged bolts or plunge into melee, trading mobility for accuracy—e.g., aiming a spear at a dodging opponent—but risk exposure to counterattacks.

Air Dash/Evade

Air dash/evade delivers a swift mid-air burst, triggered by a dash input while airborne, offering evasion or repositioning, inspired by Guilty Gear’s air dashes with a one-use limit per jump. This is the only positional tool available in the air (unless the character has traversal options like flight or a grappling hook)

  • Mechanics: Moves in the chosen direction (relative to camera), costing Energy—agile builds dash farther, heavy ones less. Speed and startup vary by build—light characters dash quicker—with a single use per jump cycle.
  • Combat Role: Dodges aerial strikes—like evading a projectile—or repositions for attacks. It pairs with aimed strikes seamlessly.
  • Tactical Use: Shifts position for flanking—e.g., dashing to a foe’s side—or escapes projectiles, balanced by Energy cost and its once-per-jump limit.

Regaining Air Options

Characters refresh aerial capabilities (jumps, dashes) through specific actions, sustaining air play.

  • Mechanics: Resets occur by landing, air-blocking or parrying a projectile (exiting stun), or escaping an air throw—no extra Energy beyond the action itself.
  • Combat Role: Allows a follow-up jump or dash—e.g., parrying a bolt to double jump—extending combos like Marvel vs. Capcom’s air chains.
  • Tactical Use: Rewards skill—e.g., parrying to leap higher—while landing resets provide recovery, balancing aerial freedom with grounded play.

Landing Recovery

Landing imposes a brief recovery period post-air, adding vulnerability.

  • Mechanics: Empty jumps restrict movement briefly (e.g., a short delay), still allowing attacks; landings after actions (e.g., dashes, strikes) enforce a fuller recovery (e.g., slightly longer, no actions)—agility shortens both, strength reduces stun.
  • Combat Role: Punishes sloppy air play—like a missed dive leaving an opening—yet permits counters if timed, akin to Smash Bros.’s landing dynamics.
  • Tactical Use: Demands smart landings—e.g., aiming for safety—or risks exposure, balanced by build resilience.

Integration with Combat and Movement

Aerial movement enhances the game’s flow:

  • Combat States: In the Directional State, jumps and dashes are omnidirectional, evading ground threats; in the Aimed State, they sharpen for precision—like a mid-air thrust.
  • Energy Dynamics: Jumps, double jumps, and dashes draw Energy—pre-jump attacks add more—preserving grounded options unless committed aloft.
  • Grounded Ties: Pre-jump flows from walking or crouching, double jumps extend dashes, linking aerial to grounded seamlessly.

Build Customization

Character builds shape aerial performance:

  • Stats: Agility boosts jump height and dash distance; strength cuts landing recovery—e.g., sturdy builds recover faster.
  • Gear Weight: Light armor enhances air speed; heavy armor limits height but bolsters resilience—e.g., less knockback.
  • Class Skills: Aerial builds (e.g., flyers) extend jump time; grapplers widen air grab range—e.g., snaring a foe mid-leap.

Challenges and Solutions

Aerial movement presents design hurdles:

  • Precision Difficulty: Air aiming might feel clunky on gamepads for ranged or melee.
    • Solution: Strengthen the soft-lock reticle and buffer inputs—ensuring seamless strikes, whether a fireball or a slash.
  • Landing Vulnerability: Recovery could overly punish aerial aggression.
    • Solution: Shorten recovery for agile builds and boost strength resilience—balancing risk with viability.
  • Balance Across Builds: Aerial-focused builds might overshadow ground ones.
    • Solution: Tweak Energy costs and stat scaling—e.g., heavy builds pay more for jumps—ensuring ground styles remain competitive.

Conclusion

Aerial movement adds a soaring dimension to The Fighting Game, blending jumping and double jumping’s versatility, pre-jump’s charged potency, and air dashes’ agility into a unified system. Rooted in Energy, stats, and combat flow, it empowers players to strike from above, dodge mid-air, or recover strategically—elevating the game’s depth with every leap and landing.

Mobility Skills: Evade/Dash/Dodge

In The Fighting Game, mobility skills—evade, dash, and dodge—are essential tools for survival and strategy, empowering players to weave through combat with precision and flair. These abilities provide fast, responsive options to avoid attacks, reposition in the arena, or set up strikes, serving as agile alternatives to static defenses like blocking. Each skill offers distinct advantages and trade-offs, shaped by Energy costs, character builds, and situational demands. Together, they create a movement system that rewards timing, foresight, and adaptability, making every step a deliberate part of the fight.

Goals of the Mobility System

The mobility skills are crafted with clear objectives in mind:

  • Defensive Flexibility: Enable players to sidestep harm or regain positioning without depending solely on shields or costly abilities.
  • Strategic Depth: Encourage thoughtful use through Energy limits and recovery periods, preventing mindless repetition.
  • Build Variety: Tie effectiveness to character traits—like agility or durability—and equipment, supporting diverse playstyles.
  • Combat Fluidity: Blend seamlessly with offensive moves and cancels, ensuring smooth shifts between attack and evasion.

Skill Overview

Mobility skills are quick, Energy-fueled maneuvers that keep players in control of the battlefield. Evade is a nimble sidestep for dodging close threats, Dash is a rapid forward burst for closing or escaping, and Dodge is a broader, more committed move for evading wide-ranging dangers. Their performance varies with a character’s build—light, agile fighters excel in speed and reach, while heavier ones sacrifice finesse for toughness—making each choice a reflection of the player’s style.

Core Mechanics

Evade

Evade is a fast, short-range shuffle, activated with a quick tap of the mobility input and a directional flick (any way but forward). It’s perfect for slipping past nearby strikes or adjusting position in a brawl.

  • Mechanics: Covers a brief span—like a swift step sideways—using a modest chunk of Energy. Agile characters glide farther, while bulkier ones take shorter hops. Its wind-up and recovery are minimal.
  • Combat Role: Dodges tight-range blows—like a fist or blade—or sets up flanking counters. It can flow into attacks, fueling quick-strike tactics.
  • Tactical Use: Shines in close quarters—think evading a punch to jab from an angle—balanced by its limited reach and Energy draw. No invulnerability frames (iframes) by default, though agility boosts might add a flicker of safety.

Dash

Dash is a sharp forward surge, triggered by a tap of the mobility key with a forward nudge, covering more ground than an evade but locking into one path.

  • Mechanics: Propels the player ahead a fair distance—faster and farther for light builds, stunted for heavy ones—draining Energy on par with an evade. Its brief recovery allows follow-up strikes, mirroring the unpredictability of a Guilty Gear dash.
  • Combat Role: Closes in for a hit—like rushing in to land a kick—or breaks away from danger. It cancels or chains into attacks.
  • Tactical Use: Excels at crossing space or dodging linear threats—say, darting through a projectile’s arc—tempered by its directional focus and Energy cost.

Dodge

Dodge is a bold, extended evasion, or dash, launched with a held press of the mobility key, spanning greater distance at the price of more energy.

  • Mechanics: Leaps a substantial stretch—farther for the nimble, shorter for the sturdy—consuming more Energy than its siblings. It’s more vulnerable towards the end of it’s animation.
  • Combat Role: Escapes sweeping attacks—like a ground-shaking blast— bridges wide gaps or closes in from a distance.
  • Tactical Use: Perfect for dodging big hazards or resetting position—imagine rolling clear of an explosion—but mistiming opens a punishable gap, offset by its Energy toll and recovery.

Dodge Crossup

When dodging toward an opponent within a range, players can teleport past, vault over, side step, or slide under the opponent, ending behind them with a split-second of invulnerability post-startup.

  • Mechanics: Costs energy. It thrives in a sweet spot of distance—too far and it would be more like just closing a gap as doge can move further that it can crossup.
  • Combat Role: Flips the fight’s geometry—like leaping over a guard for a rear strike—. Risky, as a whiff leaves you exposed briefly.
  • Tactical Use: Setting up Crossups creating confusion regarding the potential direction an attack may come from.

Integration with Combat and Builds

Mobility skills weave into the game’s fabric:

  • Energy Management: Evades sip Energy, dashes gulp a bit more, and dodges chug heartily—costs flex with build (light characters pay less, heavy ones more).
  • Attack Synergy: Most moves can cancel into mobility skills, and some mobility skills can cancel into moves.
  • Build Influence: Agility stretches range and speed; strength might toughen recovery or add iframes—e.g., a tanky dodge shrugs off a graze, an agile dash zips farther.

Challenges and Solutions

Designing mobility skills comes with hurdles:

  • Overuse Potential: Relentless dashing could cheapen spacing.
    • Solution: Tie Energy costs to build—beefy fighters drain faster—and add recovery pauses, keeping moves intentional.
  • Input Precision: Fast-paced fights might trip up directional controls.
    • Solution: Buffer inputs briefly and signal moves with visuals—like a dust trail—ensuring clarity and feel.
  • Build Balance: Agile characters might dominate movement.
    • Solution: Beef up heavy builds—say, dashes that resist staggers—leveling the field.

Conclusion

Mobility skills—evade, dash, dodge—forge a lively, tactical dance in The Fighting Game. Fueled by Energy, shaped by builds, and honed by player skill, they let you slip danger, seize openings, or outmaneuver foes. Every sidestep or leap deepens the combat’s rhythm, making movement as thrilling as the strike that follows.

Traversal Movement

Traversal governs how characters move across the arena, enhancing tactical mobility:

  • Traversal Options: Players might fly over obstacles, swing with grappling hooks, or leap to platforms—all tied to character builds or gear, drawing from superhero-inspired flair.
  • Strategic Uses: Flying evades melee foes, grappling hooks offer quick escapes, and wall-running enables flanking, each balanced by Energy costs scaled with weight and stats.
  • Build Integration: Agility boosts traversal speed (e.g., faster climbing), while strength might improve durability (e.g., resisting knockback mid-traversal), tailoring options to player choice.

Traversal transforms arenas into dynamic battlegrounds, rewarding creative navigation.

Sprint/Traversal

Sprinting and traversal enhance movement in The Fighting Game, weaving speed and environmental mastery into its high-fantasy, 3rd-person combat experience. Sprinting propels players across the arena with rapid bursts of momentum, while traversal leverages an approach inspired by Brink’s S.M.A.R.T. (Smooth Movement Across Random Terrain) system—allowing intuitive, fluid navigation over obstacles like walls, ledges, and gaps. Both mechanics are governed by Energy costs and shaped by character builds, ensuring they complement the game’s tactical pacing and enrich combat without overshadowing its core rhythm.

Goals

The sprinting and traversal system is designed to:

  • Balance Speed and Control: Provide swift movement options that enhance positioning without disrupting combat spacing, keeping sprinting tactical rather than constant.
  • Elevate Arena Interaction: Transform environments into interactive spaces, rewarding clever traversal with strategic advantages.
  • Support Build Diversity: Adjust sprint and traversal performance through stats, gear weight, and class skills, reflecting player customization.
  • Preserve Combat Focus: Position sprinting and traversal as secondary to core combat movement, maintaining deliberate, skill-driven engagement.

Overview

Sprinting accelerates ground movement for closing gaps or evading threats, draining Energy continuously—more so under attack—and introducing momentum that shifts players into a Sprinting State with unique, high-impact attacks. Traversal, inspired by Brink’s S.M.A.R.T. system, predicts player intent to seamlessly vault over obstacles, climb walls, or wall-run along ledges, adapting to the terrain without complex inputs. This approach turns arenas into dynamic, multi-level battlegrounds, where agile fighters sprint and traverse with ease, while heavier builds trade speed for durability, making every move a reflection of tactical intent and player design.

Core Mechanics

Sprinting

Sprinting significantly boosts speed, ideal for covering distance or pursuing foes, drawing inspiration from momentum-driven dashes but tempered for combat balance.

  • Mechanics: Drains Energy over time, with increased cost when under attack—light builds sustain it longer, heavy ones tire quickly. Momentum lingers after stopping, transitioning into a Skid State with reduced control, while agility enhances speed and strength mitigates knockback.
  • Combat Role: Enters a Sprinting State with momentum-charged attacks—like a ramming strike or sliding kick—offering unique offensive options. Blocking during sprinting may carry a recovery penalty, prioritizing offense over defense.
  • Tactical Use: Closes wide gaps—such as chasing a retreating opponent—or flees hazards, balanced by the risk of overshooting or exposure during the skid.

Skid State

The Skid State follows sprinting, a brief slide where momentum delays stopping, akin to sliding mechanics in fast-paced shooters but with tactical consequences.

  • Mechanics: Maintains forward motion briefly, preventing instant blocks—agility shortens the skid, strength reduces stumble risks. Costs no additional Energy beyond sprinting.
  • Combat Role: Limits immediate defense, encouraging follow-up dashes or attacks—like a sliding tackle—to maintain momentum.
  • Tactical Use: Extends sprint range but exposes players to counters, requiring foresight to avoid overextension.

Slide State

Crouching mid-sprint triggers a Slide State, a low-profile skid that dodges or engages, blending parkour fluidity with combat utility.

  • Mechanics: Reduces the hitbox and costs a small burst of Energy—light and heavy build traveling differently depending on the momentum they carry. Transitions smoothly from sprinting, ends in the crouched state.
  • Combat Role: Enables low attacks—like a sweeping kick—or evasion of high strikes, offering versatility mid-motion.
  • Tactical Use: Ducks under projectiles or sets up ground counters, balanced by its brief duration and Energy draw.

Traversal

Traversal employs a S.M.A.R.T.-inspired approach—Smooth Movement Across Random Terrain—predicting player intent to navigate obstacles fluidly, much like Brink’s system but tailored for combat integration.

  • Mechanics: Costs Energy per action—Hurddling less than vaulting less than clambering. S.M.A.R.T. pre-analyzes terrain for reachability (e.g., ledges, walls), auto-executing simple vaults and requiring manual inputs (jump, grab) for complex moves like climbing or wall-running, blending intuition with control.
  • Combat Role: Supports limited attacks mid-traversal—enhancing surprise or positioning—without dominating combat, keeping the focus on core engagement.
  • Tactical Use: Scales heights for ambushes—e.g., climbing to drop on foes—or bypasses obstacles like gaps or barriers, balanced by Energy costs and exposure risks.

Traversal State Attack Variations

Traversal state attacks turn movement into offense, offering dynamic strikes mid-action inspired by Brink’s seamless parkour-combat blend. These are conceptual possibilities, balanced by Energy costs and timing risks.

  • Vaulting Attacks:
    • Concept: Strikes mid-vault, such as a kick or weapon swing as you leap over obstacles.
    • Role: Disrupts foes below or sets up follow-ups—e.g., kicking an enemy to interrupt their stance.
  • Climbing Attacks:
    • Concept: Attacks from walls or ledges, like a downward stab or thrown object.
    • Role: Targets enemies below or harasses from height—e.g., striking a foe from a ledge perch.
  • Swinging Attacks:
    • Concept: Strikes mid-swing from ropes or hooks, such as a sweeping slash or targeted kick.
    • Role: Hits foes dynamically—e.g., slashing across a group during a swing.

These variations encourage players to merge traversal with combat, enhancing tactical flexibility while maintaining strategic depth.

Integration with Combat and Builds

Sprinting and traversal integrate with the game’s broader systems:

  • Energy Dynamics: Sprinting drains Energy continuously, traversal uses bursts—light builds conserve more, heavy ones expend faster—tying to the Energy gauge. Attacks in these states add costs, preserving balance.
  • Build Influence:
    • Agility: Boosts sprint speed and traversal fluidity—e.g., faster wall-runs.
    • Strength: Reduces knockback or Energy drain—e.g., stable sprints under attack.
    • Gear Weight: Light armor enhances speed; heavy armor bolsters resilience.
  • Combat Ties: Sprinting flows into dashes or jumps, traversal links to aerial strikes—e.g., vaulting into a dive—or prone slides, ensuring seamless transitions.

Additional Traversal Elements

The following are potential ideas to expand traversal, inspired by Brink’s S.M.A.R.T. system and prior mechanics:

  • Grappling Hooks: Swinging or pulling to ledges with an Energy cost, enhancing mobility—e.g., crossing gaps effortlessly.
  • Wall-Jumping: Bouncing between walls for vertical ascent, requiring agility—e.g., scaling tight spaces.
  • Ceiling Clings: Brief hanging for stealth or ambushes, tied to Energy—e.g., dropping from above.
  • Interactive Hazards: Triggering traps via traversal—e.g., vaulting to activate a hazard—adding tactical depth.
  • Dynamic Cover: Shifting obstacles mid-sprint—e.g., toppling debris for defense—offering strategic options.

These additions could enhance traversal’s role through class skills or arena design, balanced by Energy demands.

Challenges and Solutions

Sprinting and traversal present design considerations:

  • Sprinting Overuse: Excessive sprinting might disrupt combat pacing.
    • Solution: High Energy drain for heavy builds and momentum delays (Skid State) keep it situational.
  • Traversal Complexity: Parkour could overwhelm or feel clunky.
    • Solution: Leverage S.M.A.R.T.’s predictive navigation for simple actions, reserve manual inputs for precision, and utilize energy costs to balance and limit frequency.
  • Build Imbalance: Agile builds might dominate mobility.
    • Solution: Strength perks—e.g., knockback resistance during sprints—ensure heavy builds compete.

Conclusion

Sprinting and traversal, with traversal inspired by Brink’s S.M.A.R.T. system, elevate movement into a tactical cornerstone of The Fighting Game. Sprinting offers rapid dashes with momentum-driven strikes, while traversal—vaulting, climbing, swinging—turns arenas into vibrant stages of strategy and skill. Enhanced by attack variations and potential expansions, rooted in Energy, stats, and build diversity, these mechanics ensure every run or leap amplifies combat, rewarding players with a fluid, expressive experience.

Stealth

Stealth weaves a layer of cunning into The Fighting Game, empowering players to slip through shadows, launch ambushes, or evade detection in its high-fantasy, 3rd-person arenas. Inspired by the quiet tension of stealth classics and the tactical flair of ability-driven subterfuge, this system rewards patience, positioning, and environmental savvy without overshadowing the game’s core combat focus. Tied to movement, Energy, and character builds, stealth offers a versatile toolkit—whether you’re a rogue striking from concealment or a mage cloaked in mist.

Goals

The stealth system is crafted to:

  • Offer Tactical Choices: Enable surprise attacks, silent repositioning, or combat avoidance, enriching strategic options.
  • Reflect Build Diversity: Scale effectiveness with stats, gear, and class skills, supporting varied playstyles.
  • Maintain Balance: Keep stealth potent yet fair, limited by Energy costs, detection risks, and environmental factors.
  • Integrate Seamlessly: Enhance movement, Energy, and combat systems, ensuring a natural, cohesive feel.

Overview

Stealth operates as a gradient of concealment, activated through movement or abilities and shaped by noise, visibility, and enemy awareness. Players can crouch for subtle sneaking, prone for near-invisibility, or wield skills to vanish entirely, each draining Energy to sustain. Arenas brimming with cover—shadows, tall grass, and vertical perches—create opportunities, while open spaces challenge stealth’s limits. This system shines for initiating fights or escaping danger but falters under scrutiny, complementing the game’s direct combat rhythms.

Core Mechanics

Entering Stealth

Stealth begins with deliberate movement or specialized abilities, blending subtlety with power:

  • Crouching or Proning: Crouching muffles sound and shrinks your profile; proning maximizes concealment at the cost of speed. Both are baseline options, costing minimal Energy to maintain.
  • Abilities: Class-specific skills—like a rogue’s invisibility or a mage’s illusion—deepen stealth, drawing more Energy over time or with use.
  • Energy Drain: Quiet movement sips Energy, abilities gulp it—sprinting or attacking shatters the veil instantly.

Detection and Visibility

Detection hinges on dynamic interplay, echoing the cat-and-mouse tension of stealth-action games:

  • Noise: Slower steps and soft terrain (e.g., grass over stone) reduce sound—agility quiets further, heavy gear clanks louder.
  • Visibility: Shadows, cover, and dark attire obscure presence; bright zones or open ground expose it.
  • Enemy Awareness: Foes detect based on proximity and alertness—stealth skills shrink their range, while sharp-eyed enemies pierce the shroud.

Stealth Attacks

Stealth unlocks devastating openers, akin to precision strikes in assassination games:

  • Surprise Attacks: Strikes from hiding amplify damage—e.g., a dagger’s sting triples—or add effects like stun, breaking stealth with an Energy cost.
  • Special Moves: Abilities like a shadow-step teleport enable ambushes or escapes, balanced by Energy use and cooldowns.
  • Tactical Edge: Ideal for softening targets—e.g., silencing a caster—or retreating under cover, but risky if foes spot you first.

Integration with Game Systems

Stealth syncs with the game’s core:

  • Movement: Crouching and proning root stealth in grounded mobility—proning in shadows vanishes you, crouching behind obstacles masks approach. Traversal extends it vertically—silent climbs or vaults perch you unseen.
  • Energy: Sustaining stealth draws Energy—light builds endure longer, heavy ones tire faster—while abilities spike the cost, aligning with the resource pool.
  • Builds:
    • Stats: Agility cuts noise and Energy use; perception spots hidden foes.
    • Gear: Light armor and dark cloaks boost stealth; bulky plate hinders it.
    • Class Skills: Rogues gain silent steps or takedowns, mages cast illusions—each tailors stealth to intent.

Challenges and Solutions

Stealth poses unique hurdles:

  • Pacing Disruption: Overuse could slow fights or feel too strong.
    • Solution: Energy drain rises with activity; open spaces and alert foes curb dominance.
  • Detection Clarity: Players need feedback on stealth status.
    • Solution: A stealth meter and enemy alertness cues—like subtle glows—offer transparency.
  • Build Imbalance: Stealth builds might outshine others.
    • Solution: Counters like light-based reveals or keen senses balance it; precision demands keep it skill-based.
  • May not be suitable for 1v1: Hit and hide tactics could dominate timed matches.
    • Solution: Limit 1v1 features and preserve stronger stealth features for expanded development down the road, best suited towards group or PvE Combat situations.

Additional Context

Stealth thrives in arenas with shadows, grass, and ledges—dim corners conceal, lit plazas expose. Some builds amplify it—rogues with silent traversal, tanks with brute-force counters—while others pierce it with detection skills. It’s a choice, not a crutch, enhancing combat’s ebb and flow.

Conclusion

Stealth drapes a tactical veil over The Fighting Game’s vibrant arenas, rewarding guile and timing. Rooted in movement, Energy, and build diversity, it lets players strike from shadows or vanish in a pinch—adding tension and flair without breaking the clash of steel and sorcery. Whether a rogue’s silent blade or a mage’s misty shroud, stealth turns every hushed moment into a calculated edge.

Integration with Combat

Movement types seamlessly blend with combat mechanics:

  • Combat movement Synergy: A teleport dodge sets up a backstab, or a well timed evade for a counterpunch. Stances dynamically adjust hitboxes, adding depth to engagements.
  • Traversal in Battle: Grappling to a ledge mid-fight or dropping from a leap with an aerial strike keeps combat fluid. Flying characters might favor aerial attacks, offset by slower aiming precision.
  • Verticality: Jumping or flying introduces vertical tactics, letting players exploit height for offense or defense, balanced by Energy demands.

This integration makes movement a key combat tool, enhancing unpredictability.

Character Customization

Movement is deeply tied to character creation, offering tailored mobility:

  • Traversal Options: Skills or gear unlock flying, grappling, or leaping, matching mobility to strategy—e.g., a mage levitates, a rogue grapples.
  • Stat Influence: Agility boosts speed and dodge range, strength enhances traversal resilience, and gear weight adds trade-offs—light armor speeds up, heavy slows down.

This customization ensures every build’s movement feels distinct and purposeful.

Balancing Movement

Diverse options require careful balance:

  • Energy Management: High-impact moves like flying or teleporting drain Energy quickly, encouraging strategic use over constant reliance.
  • Stat Dependencies: Agility enhances speed, while strength offers durability benefits, ensuring balanced playstyles without over-dominance.
  • Cooldowns and Limits: Traversal tools like grappling hooks have range caps or short cooldowns, preventing excessive repositioning.

These measures keep movement fair and skill-focused, preserving combat pacing.

Environmental Interaction

Movement types enhance arena dynamics:

  • Tactical Advantages: Wall-running or grappling reaches hidden spots for ambushes, leveraging verticality.
  • Stealth Opportunities: Quiet stances or crouching enable sneaky approaches through cover like debris or grass.
  • Dynamic Elements: Sprinting might shatter weak barriers, or vaulting could trigger hazards, adding risk-reward layers.

This interplay makes arenas active spaces, rewarding clever movement.

Challenges and Solutions

The movement types system faces unique hurdles:

  • Traversal Complexity: Expansive options like flying or grappling could overwhelm early development or confuse players.
    • Solution: Start with a core set (e.g., jumping, climbing), layering advanced options (e.g., flight) later, balanced by higher Energy costs or stat requirements.
  • Speed Imbalance: Fast traversal might outpace slower stances, skewing combat fairness.
    • Solution: Cap traversal speed with Energy drain—e.g., flying costs more for heavy builds—and boost stance resilience (e.g., strength reduces knockback), ensuring parity.
  • Arena Misuse: Traversal could let players avoid combat excessively.
    • Solution: Limit traversal range or frequency—e.g., grappling hooks have a cooldown—and design arenas to encourage engagement, not evasion.

Conclusion

Movement types create a rich, strategic system that elevates the game’s combat and character depth. Fighting stances bring personality and precision to close-quarters battles, while traversal options expand the battlefield, offering creative ways to engage and outmaneuver foes. Balanced through Energy, stats, and environmental ties, this system ensures every stride or soar enhances the gladiator-inspired experience, delivering both immersion and tactical variety.

Combat and Build Integration

Movement weaves into combat seamlessly:

  • Energy Costs: Sprinting, dashing, jumping, and traversal drain Energy, scaled by build—light characters pay less, heavy ones more—ensuring strategic choices over reckless evasion.
  • State Synergy: The Directional State’s strafing flows into dashes or dogdes for reactive play, while the Aimed State’s slow steps pair with precise specials—e.g., a backpedal sets up a stalwart retreat attack.
  • Build Impact: Some stats can boost dash distance and jump height, while others might enhance knockback resistance. Gear weight slows heavy builds but bolsters resilience, tailoring movement to each fighter.

Challenges and Solutions

  • Evasion Overload: Fluid movement might favor dodging over fighting, especially for agile builds.
    • Solution: Scale Energy costs by weight and stats—e.g., heavy builds pay more for dashes, light builds drain faster under attack—balancing mobility with combat focus.
  • Precision Lag: The Aimed State’s slower pace could hinder gamepad responsiveness.
    • Solution: Add a soft-lock reticle and buffer motion inputs (5-10 frames), ensuring aimed actions feel fluid—e.g., a quarter-circle special triggers reliably.
  • Build Disparity: Heavy builds might feel sluggish compared to light ones.
    • Solution: Offset with strength benefits—e.g., heavy builds resist knockback and juggle less, light builds evade better but take more damage—keeping all styles viable.

Conclusion

Movement is a dynamic, build-driven system that turns arenas into tactical playgrounds. With Directional and Aimed States anchoring a suite of options—walking, sprinting, dashing, jumping, crouching, proning, and traversal—it empowers players to outmaneuver foes with skill and strategy. Energy costs and customization ensure every action feels deliberate and impactful, enhancing the game’s blend of fighting precision and RPG depth for thrilling, immersive duels.
