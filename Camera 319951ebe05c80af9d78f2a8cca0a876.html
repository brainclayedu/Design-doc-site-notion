Camera

Introduction

The camera in The Fighting Game is the player’s lens into its high-fantasy, 3rd-person combat world, dynamically framing the clash of blades, the arc of spells, and the sweep of sprawling arenas. Controlled by the right thumbstick on gamepads or mouse for keyboard setups, it shifts fluidly between perspectives to balance awareness and precision without relying on a hard lock-on. Enhanced by soft-lock targeting and orientation smoothing, the camera reduces the need for constant adjustments, keeping players immersed in strategy and action across both duels and broader skirmishes.

Goals

The camera system is designed to:

  • Immerse Players: Offer a cinematic view that captures the intensity of combat and the grandeur of the environment.
  • Adapt to States: Transition smoothly between a wide, awareness-driven Directional State and a focused, over-the-shoulder Aimed State.
  • Reduce Adjustments: Ease manual camera control through automatic snapping and soft targeting, enhancing flow without eliminating player agency.
  • Scale Seamlessly: Support 1v1 duels, group combat, and potential summons with flexible, responsive framing.

Overview

The camera eschews hard lock-on mechanics, favoring a soft-lock reticle and dynamic adjustments to keep combat fluid and adaptable. It shifts between:

  • Directional State: A wider, left-offset view for tracking multiple threats or navigating terrain.
  • Aimed State: A tight, over-the-shoulder perspective for precision strikes or defenses.
  • Air Aiming: A stabilized mid-flight view for aerial targeting.

A dual-reticle system—screen center and character aim—clarifies intent, while orientation smoothing aligns the character naturally with camera movement. This ensures responsiveness and clarity, syncing with the game’s Energy-driven actions and build-specific playstyles.

Core Mechanics

Camera States

The camera adapts its framing to player actions:

  • Directional State Camera:
    • Pulls back and offsets slightly left, akin to Assassin’s Creed Valhalla’s broad sweep, prioritizing situational awareness for dodging or engaging multiple foes.
    • Auto-snaps subtly toward the nearest opponent during directional attacks, reducing adjustment needs without locking focus.
  • Aimed State Camera:
    • Zooms in over the shoulder, echoing God of War 2018’s intimacy, sharpening focus for aimed attacks or parries.
    • A soft-lock reticle highlights nearby targets within a flexible radius, easing precision on gamepads or keyboards.
  • Air Aiming Camera:
    • Slows the jump arc briefly when aiming, stabilizing the view—like Overwatch’s aerial precision—for mid-flight strikes or spells, balancing exposure with control.

Reticle and Orientation

Targeting and movement are clarified through:

  • Dual Reticle:
    • Features a screen-center reticle (camera aim) and a character-aim reticle, lagging slightly due to turn speed limits, similar to Helldivers 2. This keeps attack direction clear.
    • A soft-lock radius snaps to close foes, tightening as threats approach, streamlining targeting without rigidity.
  • Orientation Smoothing:
    • Aligns character facing with camera motion gradually, snapping within a set angle for responsiveness—agility speeds this, heavy builds slow it.
    • Attacks lock the initial direction, with recovery frames realigning to the camera, ensuring natural flow over chaotic spins.

Camera in Combat

Combat dynamics shape camera behavior:

  • Soft Lock and Auto-Orient:
    • A soft-lock reticle guides focus to the nearest or most threatening foe during attacks or defenses, inspired by For Honor’s adaptability, avoiding hard lock-on constraints.
    • Smoothing prevents disorientation in group fights, with a brief buffer for facing adjustments.
  • Group Combat Scalability:
    • The Directional State’s wide view tracks multiple enemies, while soft targeting prioritizes key threats—ideal for summons or allies in future expansions.
    • Reduces adjustment needs as foes shift, keeping the battlefield legible without manual panning.

Modern Combat Inspirations

Future development may draw from modern examples like God of War: Ragnarök and Assassin’s Creed Valhalla:

  • Valhalla: Switches between a wide melee view and a tighter bow-aiming stance, offering a model for blending directional and aimed combat fluidly.
  • Ragnarök: Acts as a hybrid, balancing melee precision with ranged options (e.g., axe throws), suggesting ways to merge agility and power in camera framing.

    These approaches could refine how the camera handles diverse combat styles, enhancing its adaptability.

Integration with Movement and Builds

The camera syncs with core systems:

  • Movement:
    • Widens during sprints or wall-running for broad awareness, tightens for stealth crouching or proning to focus on subtlety—reflecting pace and intent.
    • Stabilizes briefly in aerial aiming, then tracks jump arcs for smooth transitions—agility enhances this fluidity.
  • Energy:
    • No direct Energy cost, but movement-driven shifts (e.g., dashes, jumps) influence framing—light builds adjust faster, heavy ones lag slightly.
  • Builds:
    • Agility: Speeds camera snapping, boosting responsiveness—e.g., quick pans for rogues.
    • Perception: Extends the soft-lock range, sharpening awareness for tactical builds.
    • Gear: Light armor widens the field subtly; heavy armor narrows it for a grounded focus—enhancing immersion.

Challenges and Solutions

Camera design presents hurdles:

  • Awareness vs. Precision: Wide views might miss details; tight ones lose context.
    • Solution: State-driven shifts—wide for awareness, tight for focus—guided by a soft-lock reticle balance both needs.
  • Responsiveness Lag: Slow turns could disrupt fast combat.
    • Solution: Agility accelerates turn speed; a buffered reticle ensures attacks align with intent, not just facing—e.g., a swing hits where aimed.
  • Build Imbalance: Agile builds might exploit camera agility.
    • Solution: Strength adds stability—e.g., steady aim under pressure—keeping heavy builds competitive.

Additional Context

The camera thrives in arenas rich with verticality and shadows—broad views reveal ledges or traps, tight focus hones strikes or stealth moves. Class skills tweak its feel—stealth dims edges for ambiance, aerial widens mid-air shots—while Energy-driven actions like sprints or dashes shift its perspective, deepening the game’s immersive flow.

Conclusion

The camera in The Fighting Game is a dynamic, responsive lens that frames every clash and quiet step with cinematic precision. By reducing adjustment needs through soft targeting and smoothing, and adapting seamlessly between states, it keeps players immersed—whether weaving through a duel or scanning a chaotic horde. Inspired by modern combat design, it offers a foundation for tactical brilliance, amplifying every build and battlefield moment.

Camera Reticle / Character Turn Speed

The camera reticle and character turn speed in The Fighting Game form the backbone of its 3rd-person targeting and movement precision, bridging player intent with in-game action. This system uses a dual-reticle design to clarify aim and orientation, while turn speed governs how swiftly characters align with the camera—together ensuring combat feels responsive yet grounded. Designed to support fluid navigation and tactical engagement without a hard lock-on, it balances awareness, accuracy, and character responsiveness in a dynamic, high-fantasy battlefield.

Goals

The reticle and turn speed system is crafted to:

  • Clarify Targeting: Provide intuitive visual cues for aiming attacks or tracking foes, reducing guesswork in chaotic fights.
  • Balance Fluidity and Realism: Sync character movement with camera control naturally, avoiding instant pivots while keeping pace with combat demands.
  • Enhance Tactical Depth: Support varied playstyles—melee precision, ranged accuracy, or stealthy evasion—through adjustable responsiveness.
  • Reduce Control Burden: Ease targeting and turning effort via soft-lock mechanics and smoothing, maintaining focus on strategy over micromanagement.

Overview

The camera reticle employs a dual design: one marking the screen center (camera aim), the other indicating character aim, which lags slightly due to turn speed limits. Inspired by Helldivers 2’s split-reticle approach, this system ensures attacks align with intent, not just raw camera position. Turn speed governs how quickly characters pivot to match the camera, influenced by agility and build traits—swift for nimble rogues, deliberate for armored tanks. Together, they create a seamless flow between aiming, moving, and striking, tailored to Energy-driven actions and arena dynamics.

Core Mechanics

Camera Reticle

The dual-reticle system splits focus between camera and character:

  • Screen Center Reticle: Marks where the camera points, driven by mouse or right thumbstick input—akin to Overwatch’s crosshair—guiding overall view.
  • Character Aim Reticle: Indicates where the character faces and attacks, lagging slightly behind camera turns, reflecting turn speed limits. A soft-lock radius highlights nearby foes, snapping focus subtly like God of War’s gentle targeting assist.
  • Combat Role: Ensures attacks hit where aimed—e.g., a fireball tracks the character reticle, not just screen center—clarifying intent in melee or ranged exchanges.
  • Tactical Use: The soft-lock radius tightens on closer threats, aiding precision—e.g., targeting a sprinting foe—while broader awareness tracks distant enemies.

Character Turn Speed

Turn speed dictates how fast characters pivot to align with the camera, blending realism with responsiveness:

  • Mechanics: Base speed is moderate—agility accelerates it (e.g., quick for light builds), while heavy gear or armor slows it (e.g., deliberate for tanks). Smoothing prevents jarring spins, with a snap threshold for close alignment.
  • Combat Role: Attacks lock to the initial aim reticle direction, releasing with recovery frames that realign—similar to Dark Souls’s deliberate facing but faster-paced.
  • Tactical Use: Balances rapid reaction—e.g., turning to parry a flank—with build weight, ensuring agile characters pivot swiftly and heavy ones hold ground.

Integration with Movement

The system syncs with movement states:

  • Grounded: Walking or sprinting widens the reticle gap—agile builds close it faster—while crouching/proning narrows it for stealth precision.
  • Aerial: Jumping or air dashes adjust the character reticle dynamically—e.g., aiming mid-leap—supported by air aiming’s stabilized view.
  • Traversal: Vaulting or climbing shifts focus to terrain—e.g., a wall-run aligns the aim reticle ahead—easing navigation.

Energy and Build Influence

Energy and builds shape responsiveness:

  • Energy: No direct cost, but Energy-fueled moves (e.g., dashes) speed up turn alignment—light builds turn quicker mid-dash.
  • Builds:
    • Agility: Tightens the reticle gap and boosts turn speed—e.g., a rogue pivots faster than a knight.
    • Strength: Stabilizes turns against knockback—e.g., a tank resists mid-sprint disruption.
    • Gear: Light armor widens the soft-lock radius; heavy armor focuses it tighter—enhancing build feel.

Integration with Combat and Systems

The reticle and turn speed weave into core mechanics:

  • Combat States:
    • Directional: Wider reticle supports strafing or group targeting—e.g., tracking multiple foes.
    • Aimed: Tight reticle hones precision—e.g., lining up a skillshot—while turn speed adjusts for melee or ranged focus.
  • Stealth: Reticle dims or shifts subtly—e.g., a smaller radius for prone stealth—enhancing ambush clarity without breaking cover.
  • Traversal: Reticle tracks movement—e.g., vaulting aims forward—while turn speed ensures smooth shifts mid-action.

Challenges and Solutions

Design hurdles include:

  • Targeting Ambiguity: Reticle lag might confuse attack direction in fast fights.
    • Solution: Buffer attack inputs to align with the aim reticle, ensuring strikes hit where intended—e.g., a swing follows the reticle, not facing.
  • Turn Speed Clunkiness: Slow turns could lag behind rapid combat needs.
    • Solution: Agility scales turn speed dynamically—e.g., quick pivots for light builds—and smoothing softens transitions for heavy ones.
  • Group Combat Overload: Multiple foes might overwhelm targeting.
    • Solution: Soft-lock prioritizes nearest threats, with a wider radius for agile builds—e.g., tracking a charging foe over a distant caster.

Additional Context

The system thrives in arenas with verticality and hazards—wide views reveal ledges, tight reticles target foes across gaps. Class skills tweak it—stealth dims the reticle for subtlety, perception widens it for awareness—while Energy moves like sprints or dashes shift its pace, deepening immersion and strategy.

Conclusion

The camera reticle and character turn speed in The Fighting Game fuse precision and fluidity, guiding every strike and pivot with clarity. Through dual reticles, soft targeting, and build-driven responsiveness, they bridge player intent with battlefield chaos—ensuring a rogue’s stealthy turn or a warrior’s steady aim feels natural and impactful. This system elevates combat’s tactical flow, framing each moment with purpose and poise.
